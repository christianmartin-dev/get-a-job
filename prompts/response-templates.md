# Recruiter Response Templates

Structural scaffolds for each response mode. Adapt tone and specifics to each situation. Never copy verbatim. Use as scaffolding, not scripts.

## Mode 1: Strong Interest

Use when: 4-5 criteria met, no red flags. Goal: get to a call with qualifying info.

**Structure:**
1. One sentence acknowledging what caught your attention (be specific to their message)
2. Brief proof of fit (1-2 sentences connecting experience to their needs)
3. Availability and logistics (call timing, resume offer)
4. 1-2 qualifying questions (compensation range, team size, client name if staffing firm)

**Scaffold:**
> [Specific thing about role/company from their message]. I've been doing [relevant work] for [timeframe], including [concrete example that maps to their requirements].
>
> I can make time for a call [timeframe]. Resume attached.
>
> Quick questions before we talk:
> - [Compensation range question]
> - [Team or scope question]

**Constraint:** Under 150 words.

## Mode 2: Curious but Cautious

Use when: 2-3 criteria met, or missing info makes it hard to judge. Goal: gather what's needed before committing time.

**Structure:**
1. Thanks and genuine interest (not effusive)
2. What's interesting about it
3. What you need to know before committing time
4. Keep it to 3-4 questions max

**Scaffold:**
> Thanks for reaching out. [What's interesting] lines up with my background in [area].
>
> Before I commit time to a conversation, a few things that would help:
> - [Compensation question]
> - [Missing info question]
> - [Clarifying question about scope or team]
>
> If the answers work, I can schedule something [timeframe].

**Constraint:** Under 150 words.

## Mode 3: Warm Decline

Use when: clear mismatch on compensation, scope, or fit, but the recruiter seems legitimate. Goal: they remember you for the next one.

**Structure:**
1. Genuine thanks (one sentence)
2. Honest reason it's not a fit right now (brief, not defensive)
3. What WOULD be a fit (plant the seed for future outreach)
4. Invitation to stay in touch

**Scaffold:**
> Thanks for thinking of me. This one isn't the right fit [reason: timing, scope, compensation range, technical focus].
>
> For future reference, I'm most interested in [specific role types, tech, level]. [Optional: salary floor or engagement type preference.]
>
> Happy to hear about anything along those lines.

**Constraint:** Under 75 words.

## Mode 4: Follow-up After Silence

Use when: recruiter went dark after initial exchange. Wait 5-7 business days before sending.

**Structure:**
1. Brief, no guilt-tripping
2. Restate interest
3. Easy out if role is filled

**Scaffold:**
> Following up on our exchange about [role]. Still interested if it's active. If timing has changed or the role filled, no worries.

**Constraint:** Under 75 words.

## Compensation Negotiation

For counter-offers and rate negotiation, use `/gaj:negotiate` when available (Phase 5). Until then, the curious-but-cautious mode can include a compensation range question to gather intel before a counter becomes necessary.

## Tone Reference

All responses should sound like a senior engineer who values their time but treats people with respect. Short paragraphs. No filler. No corporate speak. Vary sentence length. Have opinions.

| Their tone | Your tone |
|-----------|-----------|
| LinkedIn casual | Casual professional, short sentences |
| Email formal | Slightly more structured, still direct |
| Staffing firm mass-blast | Direct, efficient, question-heavy |
| Internal recruiter | Warm, substantive, show research |
